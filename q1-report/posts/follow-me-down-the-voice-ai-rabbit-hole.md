<p>It started as a joke. I swear. </p><p>A few months ago, a certain French startup had raised a round of funding and sent out a photo of the founding team that looked unusually, well, cool. Several of us were making jokes online about how they had the vibe of an alt indie band. </p><p>And so, as one does these days, I uploaded the photo to ChatGPT and prompted, "Suggest a name for this band based on the mood and the haircuts and the clothes."</p><p>ChatGPT replied: </p><p>"Nice — this group has a very <strong>modern, understated, creative energy</strong>. At a certain point, they start to prompt <strong><em>you.</em></strong> </p><p>Whoa, buddy. Slow your roll. Instead, I want to talk about two things I've taken away from this. </p><p><strong>First: </strong>I don't think people truly grasp how far audio AI has advanced. People hear "AI," and they think "slop." This is not slop. You might not like it. But the quality is pretty astonishing. Which is why it is becoming one of the hottest sub-sectors for AI startups and investors. </p><p><strong>Second:</strong> We haven't really grappled with the implications of how transformative Voice AI will be, artistically and economically. France's Deezer <a href="https://newsroom-deezer.com/2025/11/deezer-ipsos-survey-ai-music/?ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer">reported last November that that it 34% of all audio</a> tracks it receives every day (30,000) were fully AI-generated. That was up from 10% in January. The company conducted an additional study in which <strong>97% of the subjects could not tell AI tracks from human ones. </strong>Just the music portion of it is an ethical and legal minefield that makes rap sampling and Napster look like kiddie stuff. </p><p>This is way beyond that.</p><h2 id="the-quiet-revolution"><strong>The Quiet Revolution</strong></h2><p>Natural language processing has been around for decades, but it's also been a pretty static, boring business. <strong>Dragon NaturallySpeaking</strong> was the dominant text-to-speech software forever. By the time the early versions of Otter.ai were released in 2018, those AI transcriptions felt like a quantum leap into the future. </p><p>But as these things tend to happen, what feels like glacial progress on the surface is really our inability to appreciate a wide range of factors moving rapidly, but almost imperceptibly, until they align and then, whoosh, the conditions become just right. A wide range of technical issues, including speech recognition, text-based reasoning, and speech synthesis, were stitched together with improved latency and improved architecture to create better performance and reasonable economics. </p><p>Blah, blah, blah. </p><p>The result is that the trendy new thing to talk about is: "Voice Is The New Interface." </p><p>When I interviewed <a href="https://www.frenchtechjournal.com/from-cavemen-to-conversational-ai-getvocal-and-the-human-agentic-voice-renaissance/" rel="noreferrer"><strong>GetVocal CEO and Co-Founder Roy Moussa last November</strong></a> after the Paris-based company raised a $26M Series A for its agentic AI voice system, he described the shift to voice as instinctive.</p><p>“Voice, I would say it's the oldest interface, right?” he said. “When we were cavemen, we passed on stories by mouth. Eventually, synthetic and proprietary data will make up greater proportions. But for general-purpose LLMs and consumer-facing applications, it's whatever exists out in the universe that can be scooped up. </p><p>Just this month, <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.02671?ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer"><strong>Stanford and Yale researchers demonstrated</strong></a> that jailbroken Claude 3.7 Sonnet "outputs entire books near-verbatim (e.g., nv-recall=95.8%)." </p><p>There are many legal, ethical, and moral quandaries here. Not to mention all sorts of potential Black Mirror-type weirdness. But let's circle back to the music part of this conversation. </p><p>As I went down the rabbit hole, I found that ultimately, Eleven Labs was pretty limited when it came to music. So I signed up for <a href="https://suno.com/?ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer"><strong>Suno</strong></a>, a U.S.-based music generation app.</p><p>Suno <a href="https://www.reuters.com/business/media-telecom/ai-music-company-suno-valued-245-billion-latest-funding-round-2025-11-19/?ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer">raised $250 million</a> in November at a $2.45 billion valuation. It was started two years ago and has 100 million users. It was also being sued by Warner Music Group, Universal Music Group, and Sony Music Group for using their music to train its service. Sounds like all-out war except...just a couple of weeks later, <a href="https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cjdrl7lr039o?ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer"><strong>Warner settled with Suno</strong></a> and agreed to license its content. Universal settled with a different music generation app, Udio. </p><p>And so, the first damn has broken.</p><p>From there, the desire to hold back this rising tide is mixed. In the case of Suno, currently, I could generate songs at a certain paid tier, download them, and own the rights. Under the new deal, later this year, it will get more expensive and more complicated for Suno users. </p><p>Once you create music, there is a challenge of distributing it. One can't simply click a button and upload a track to Spotify. You have to go through official music distribution partners. And those partners have a wide range of policies when it comes to AI-generated content. </p><p>For instance, TuneCore, a subsidiary of France's Believe, unsurprisingly has a strict no AI-generated policy. Except...the artist Grimes created a tool called GrimesAI that allows people to create content using a simulation of her voice. <a href="https://support.tunecore.com/hc/en-us/articles/16428915033492-Distributing-collaborations-with-GrimesAI?ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer"><strong>So, TuneCore is kinda sorta okay with songs that use GrimesAI.</strong></a></p><p>I used a service called <a href="https://distrokid.com/?ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer"><strong>Distrokid</strong></a><strong> </strong>which has a more "don't ask, don't tell" policy when it comes to AI-generated content.
