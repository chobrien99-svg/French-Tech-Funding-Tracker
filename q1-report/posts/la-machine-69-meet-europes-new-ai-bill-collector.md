<p>🗞️ <strong>The New Mayor of Paris, Emmanuel Grégoire, wants to build a second Station F… but make it AI.</strong> The newly elected Paris mayor is proposing a massive “Station IA” hub in the north of the city, designed to bring together startups, researchers, and public data under one roof. The goal: turn Paris into a global capital for “AI for the public good,” with access to compute power, datasets, and real-world testing through the city itself. It’s part tech strategy, part political statement in a bid to position Paris as a more ethical counterweight to Silicon Valley. | <a href="https://www.maddyness.com/2026/03/22/emmanuel-gregoire-veut-un-deuxieme-station-f-a-paris/?ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer"><strong>Maddyness</strong></a></p><p>🗞️ <strong>AI is starting to show up in the French economy, but much more quietly than in the U.S. </strong>While American growth is being noticeably boosted by a surge in digital investment, France is seeing only a modest lift so far, with AI-related spending contributing just a small fraction to GDP growth, according to the latest quarterly report from <a href="https://www.insee.fr/fr/statistiques/8907419?sommaire=8907467&ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer"><strong>national statistics agency INSEE</strong></a>. Where things get interesting is on the supply side: in both France and the U.S., AI is already reshaping the labor market, with productivity rising in tech-related sectors even as employment declines, especially for younger workers entering the field. Adoption is happening fast in certain industries like tech, finance, and consulting, but remains uneven across the broader economy. The bottom line: AI’s macro impact in France is still early and fairly muted, but beneath the surface, it’s already beginning to rewire how work gets done and who gets hired. | <a href="https://www.insee.fr/fr/statistiques/8907419?sommaire=8907467&ref=frenchtechjournal.com" rel="noreferrer"><strong>INSEE</strong></a></p><p>🗞️ <strong>Mistral AI’s independence is starting to look… expensive. </strong>The French AI champion is leaning heavily on Nvidia for compute power, raising questions about how “sovereign” Europe’s flagship model maker really is. The proposal adds fuel to an already heated debate over how to fairly share value in the generative AI economy. dysfunctions of European business. Everyone complains about them, yet few actors seem to address them.</p><p>Interestingly, late payments are simultaneously improving in Europe while worsening.&nbsp; </p><p>According to France’s payments observatory (<em>l’observatoire des délais de paiement</em>), a majority of SMEs and micro-enterprises now pay within the legal 60-day window. Yet around 30% of companies still pay late, and only half of large firms settle invoices on time. More strikingly, average delays climbed back to 13.6 days in late 2024, now above the European average (12.1 days in 2024).</p><p>Fewer offenders. Bigger delays.</p><p>That contradiction sits at the heart of a growing fintech opportunity - and it’s exactly where Paris-based startup Cleavr is positioning itself.</p><p>“It’s very heterogeneous,” says Baptiste Nassoy, co-founder and CEO of Cleavr. “You have companies that are more disciplined - but the ones who are late, are <em>really</em> late.”</p><p>Welcome to accounts receivable, the last operational <em>casse tête</em> AI hasn’t properly touched.
